================================================================================
UKRAINIAN AIR-RAID ALERT ANALYSIS — CONCLUSIONS
Dataset: Vadimkin/ukrainian-air-raid-sirens-dataset (official feed, oblast level)
Trustworthy analysis window: 2022-03-15 to 2025-10-31
Prepared: project working report
================================================================================

This document states, as honestly as possible, what the analysis supports,
what it does not, where the data was sufficient to draw a conclusion and where
it was not, and how these results can feed further work. It is organised as:

  PART 1 — Plain-language summary (no jargon)
  PART 2 — What we are confident about
  PART 3 — What is uncertain or inconclusive
  PART 4 — Data-quality issues (read this before trusting any number)
  PART 5 — Methods and key metrics (technical detail)
  PART 6 — How these conclusions can be used / next steps

A one-line honest verdict up front:
  This data is EXCELLENT for describing and detecting patterns, and POOR for
  forecasting the timing of individual alerts. Both halves of that statement
  are backed by metrics in this report, not asserted.


================================================================================
PART 1 — PLAIN-LANGUAGE SUMMARY
================================================================================

We analysed roughly four years of official air-raid alerts for Ukraine, broken
down by region (oblast). After cleaning the data we could reliably study the
period from mid-March 2022 to the end of October 2025.

What we learned, in plain terms:

* Alerts follow a clear daily rhythm. They are least frequent in the early
  morning (around 5-7 a.m. local time) and most frequent around midday, with a
  second rise in the evening. This pattern is strong and consistent when you
  look at the whole country.

* Alerts are heavily concentrated in front-line and eastern/southern regions
  (Donetsk, Zaporizhzhia, Kharkiv, Dnipropetrovsk). Western regions get an
  order of magnitude fewer.

* The overall intensity of the war rose and fell over time, peaking in roughly
  early-to-mid 2024.

* Some symbolic dates show more alerts than usual — most reliably New Year and,
  to a lesser degree, the Western-calendar Christmas (25 Dec) and Women's Day
  (8 March). Surprisingly, the anniversary of the invasion (24 February) tends
  to have FEWER alerts than the surrounding weeks, not more.

* We could NOT predict when an individual alert will happen. Knowing the day of
  week, month, or hour barely helps guess tomorrow's alerts in Kyiv. This is
  expected: alerts are driven by an adversary's decisions, not a natural cycle.

* We CAN, however, reliably flag unusually intense days after the fact, and we
  can describe the patterns above with confidence.


================================================================================
PART 2 — WHAT WE ARE CONFIDENT ABOUT (well-supported by the data)
================================================================================

These conclusions rest on large sample sizes (tens of thousands of alerts) and
are stable across the trustworthy window.

2.1 DIURNAL (HOUR-OF-DAY) SEASONALITY — HIGH CONFIDENCE
    There is a robust hour-of-day pattern in Kyiv local time: a trough at
    ~05:00-07:00 (~2.2% of alerts per hour) rising to a midday peak (~12:00,
    ~5.4%) with a secondary evening elevation near 21:00. Alerts are ~2.5x more
    likely at midday than at dawn. Based on ~65,000 alerts; not a small-sample
    artifact.

2.2 REGIONAL CONCENTRATION — HIGH CONFIDENCE
    Alert counts are dominated by front-line/eastern-southern oblasts: Donetsk
    (~6,900), Zaporizhzhia (~6,700), Kharkiv (~6,500), Dnipropetrovsk (~5,600).
    Western oblasts (Lviv, Ivano-Frankivsk, Zakarpattia) sit near ~700 each.
    This is a structural feature of the conflict's geography.

2.3 WAR-PHASE TREND (NON-STATIONARITY) — HIGH CONFIDENCE
    Daily alert volume is strongly non-stationary. The STL trend component
    shows clear phases: high mid-2022, a winter-2022/23 lull, a climb to a peak
    around early-mid 2024 (busiest month March 2024, ~2,200 alerts; busiest
    single day 2022-08-24 with 189), then a gradual decline through 2025.

2.4 ANOMALY DETECTION WORKS — HIGH CONFIDENCE (as a descriptive/after-the-fact
    tool)
    Flagging days whose count is unusual RELATIVE TO LOCAL TREND + WEEKLY
    SEASON (STL-residual rolling z-score, |z|>=3) reliably surfaces real
    large-scale strike days (e.g. 2022-08-24, 2023-08-25, 2023-03-14). 37 such
    days flagged across all oblasts. This is detection, not prediction.

2.5 DURATION IS HEAVY-TAILED — HIGH CONFIDENCE
    Non-censored alert durations: median ~40 min, p90 ~182 min, p99 ~540 min.
    Front-line and some western oblasts show higher medians (~60 min) than
    central ones (~33 min).


================================================================================
PART 3 — WHAT IS UNCERTAIN OR INCONCLUSIVE (data thin or signal weak)
================================================================================

3.1 HOLIDAY / SPECIAL-DATE EFFECTS — SUGGESTIVE, NOT CONCLUSIVE
    Only 3-4 occurrences exist per holiday across the window, so these are
    directional patterns, not statistically established effects. With that
    caveat, using a +/-5-day window vs the same-year local baseline:

      ROBUST (every year agreed in direction):
        - New Year (1 Jan):        ~+28%   (elevated)
        - Invasion Anniv (24 Feb): ~-11%   (SUPPRESSED, every year below)

      PROBABLE BUT NOISIER:
        - Western Christmas (25 Dec): ~+19% (one year slightly negative)
        - Women's Day (8 Mar):        ~+8%

      NO DETECTABLE EFFECT (treat as "no evidence", not "proven zero"):
        - Orthodox Christmas (7 Jan), both Easters (Western & Orthodox Pascha),
          9 May, Independence Day (24 Aug), Defenders Day, Constitution Day.

    Honest notes:
      * The New Year elevation is the single most credible holiday finding (it
        survives both +/-3 and +/-5 day windows).
      * The Western vs Orthodox Christmas split is interesting and plausibly
        real (Ukraine officially moved Christmas to 25 Dec from 2023), but
        rests on only 3 years.
      * The 24 Feb SUPPRESSION contradicts the common intuition that
        anniversaries draw heavier attacks. It is consistent across years but
        still only 3 data points. Do not over-claim.
      * Window width matters: +/-3 days captures sharp single-day spikes;
        +/-5 days captures sustained campaigns but dilutes single spikes. We
        report +/-5; effects shrink modestly vs +/-3.

3.2 FORECASTING ALERT TIMING — CONCLUSIVELY LIMITED (this IS a firm finding,
    just a negative one)
    We can state with confidence that calendar-based forecasting of individual
    alert timing does NOT work here:
      * Daily Kyiv City count forecast: a Negative-Binomial GLM on calendar +
        recent-activity features did NOT beat naive baselines (MAE ~0.90 vs
        ~0.87 for seasonal-naive; mean of series ~1.3/day). Predictions are off
        by ~two-thirds of the mean.
      * Hourly Kyiv City P(>=1 alert): logistic model AUC ~0.57 (barely above
        chance 0.50), Brier ~0.055 essentially tied with a constant base-rate
        guess.
    The reason is structural: onsets are adversary-driven, and the hour-of-day
    seasonality, while real in aggregate, is itself non-stationary (the test
    period developed a late-evening peak absent from training).

3.3 SINGLE-REGION SEASONALITY AT FINE GRAIN — INSUFFICIENT SIGNAL
    The diurnal pattern that is strong nationally is too weak/noisy to exploit
    at the Kyiv-City hourly level on held-out future data. Aggregate signal !=
    per-city-hour predictability.

3.4 LUHANSK — NO DATA
    Only 1 record in the entire official oblast-level set. Excluded from all
    per-region analysis. No conclusions possible.

3.5 PRE-15-MARCH-2022 AND POST-OCT-2025 — OUT OF SCOPE
    The official feed starts 2022-03-15 (misses the first ~3 weeks of the
    full-scale invasion) and degrades after Oct 2025 (see Part 4). Nothing in
    this report speaks to those periods.


================================================================================
PART 4 — DATA-QUALITY ISSUES (READ BEFORE TRUSTING ANY NUMBER)
================================================================================

These are not minor footnotes; each one would have corrupted results if missed.

4.1 MASSIVE DUPLICATION (publisher-side artifact) — HANDLED
    ~50% of raw oblast-level rows were EXACT duplicates (every affected alert
    appeared exactly twice, byte-for-byte). Investigation showed the file is
    effectively one history block concatenated with a second copy up to
    ~2026-01-24, with only the most recent rows appearing once. This is a
    daily-regeneration pipeline artifact on the publisher's side, NOT two real
    reports of one alert. We removed them via full-row dedup, which is provably
    lossless (two records identical down to the second cannot be distinct
    alerts). IMPORTANT: this must remain a permanent pipeline step — a future
    refresh could change the duplication pattern.

4.2 FEED COLLAPSE AFTER OCT 2025 — TRUNCATED
    From November 2025 the feed degrades sharply; from December 2025 ONLY Kyiv
    City reports any alerts and all 24 other oblasts go silent. This is not a
    real-world ceasefire — it is a collection failure. We hard-cut the analysis
    at VALID_END = 2025-10-31. Any apparent "drop to near-zero" in late 2025 is
    an artifact. Do not interpret it as reduced attacks.

4.3 RIGHT-CENSORED DURATIONS (missed "all-clear") — FLAGGED, NOT DELETED
    When no end-of-alert signal was recorded, duration is artificially
    extended. At oblast level only 13 alerts exceed 24h, and ALL are in
    front-line/border oblasts (Kharkiv, Donetsk, Chernihiv, Sumy, Luhansk)
    where a genuinely prolonged threat state is plausible. We flagged these
    (censored=True) and EXCLUDED them from duration statistics, but KEPT their
    onset in all count statistics. Counts are therefore robust to this issue;
    duration stats are computed on non-censored alerts only.

4.4 TIMEZONE HANDLING — EXPLICIT
    Raw timestamps are UTC. All hour-of-day / day-of-week analysis uses Kyiv
    local time (Europe/Kyiv, DST-aware), because human/operational rhythms
    follow local time. NOTE: CSV round-trips silently drop timezone metadata;
    downstream code must re-localise. (Requires the `tzdata` package on systems
    lacking the IANA database.)

4.5 GRANULARITY CHOICE — DELIBERATE
    We used oblast level only. The raw file also has raion/hromada rows, but
    these double-count the same physical alert and contain the extreme
    duration outliers. Oblast is the consistent unit for regional comparison.
    Intra-oblast hotspot analysis would require revisiting this.

4.6 OVERLAPPING ALERTS — NONE (but guarded)
    After dedup, zero overlapping intervals per oblast remain. A defensive
    merge step is retained in case a future refresh introduces them.


================================================================================
PART 5 — METHODS AND KEY METRICS (technical detail)
================================================================================

PIPELINE (4 phases, modular Python):
  ingest.py    load -> oblast filter -> full-row dedup -> drop-invalid ->
               defensive overlap-merge -> derive duration/censored/Kyiv-local
  eda.py       daily/monthly counts, hour-of-day & day-of-week shares,
               regional breakdown, duration distribution, STL decomposition
  anomaly.py   STL-residual rolling z-score anomaly detection; holiday windows
               vs same-year local baseline (fixed + movable feasts)
  forecast.py  Kyiv City daily count (NB GLM vs baselines) and hourly P(alert)
               (logistic), strict time-based split

PRIMARY METRIC CHOICE:
  We analyse ALERT ONSET COUNTS as the main signal, not durations. Counts are
  robust to the censoring problem (a missed all-clear inflates duration, not
  the fact an alert started).

SEASONALITY / DECOMPOSITION:
  STL with period=7 (weekly). Annual seasonality is weak/irregular
  (adversary-driven), so the weekly component is the meaningful periodic term.
  ADF on Kyiv City daily series: stationary at city level (p<0.001), but the
  national aggregate trend is strongly non-stationary — both are true at
  different aggregation levels.

ANOMALY DETECTION:
  STL removes trend+weekly season; remainder standardised by a 30-day rolling
  local std (handles changing volatility across war phases); flag |z|>=3. A
  global z-score was rejected because it would mislabel the busy 2024 period
  wholesale.

HOLIDAY ANALYSIS:
  +/-5-day window vs same-year +/-21-day local baseline (window excluded).
  Pooling across years against a GLOBAL mean is invalid because the war-phase
  trend confounds it. Movable feasts (Western Easter, Orthodox Pascha) use
  explicit per-year dates. n=3-4 per holiday — the binding limitation.

FORECAST EVALUATION:
  Strict time-based split (most-recent 20% held out; no shuffling).
  Daily: count-appropriate metrics (MAE, RMSE, Poisson deviance); NB GLM
  because mean-1.3 overdispersed counts make Gaussian ARIMA inappropriate.
  Hourly: 95% of hours empty -> model P(>=1 alert), evaluate with ROC-AUC and
  Brier score, not point accuracy.
  Baselines: global mean and day-of-week seasonal-naive (daily); constant
  base-rate (hourly).

KEY NUMBERS:
  Clean alerts: 65,128 | regions: 25 (24 usable) | window: 2022-03-15..2025-10-31
  Daily Kyiv City: mean 1.32, var 1.86 (overdispersion 1.41), 32% zero-days
  Hourly Kyiv City: 95% zero-hours, base rate ~5.4%
  Daily forecast MAE: seasonal-naive 0.865 vs NB 0.895 (NB does not win)
  Hourly AUC 0.569; Brier 0.0551 (vs 0.0553 baseline)


================================================================================
PART 6 — HOW THESE CONCLUSIONS CAN BE USED / NEXT STEPS
================================================================================

WHAT THESE RESULTS ARE GOOD FOR:
  * Situational description & briefing: the diurnal curve, regional ranking,
    and war-phase trend are solid enough to cite in analytical summaries.
  * Retrospective anomaly flagging: the STL-residual detector is a usable tool
    for "which days/regions were unusually intense relative to their baseline".
  * Baselines for any future model: the naive benchmarks here set the bar any
    real forecaster must beat. (Calendar-only models do not.)
  * Hypothesis generation: the holiday signals (New Year up, 24 Feb down) are
    worth testing against external strike-event datasets.

WHAT THEY SHOULD NOT BE USED FOR:
  * Predicting when an individual alert will occur. The data does not support
    it, and we measured exactly how badly calendar features perform.
  * Reading anything into the post-Oct-2025 data (collection failure).
  * Duration conclusions that include censored alerts.

NEXT STEP #1 (highest value): SPATIAL NOWCASTING
  Rationale: the published research on this dataset found a region's alert
  state is best predicted by its NEIGHBOURS' current state — alerts sweep
  geographically as drones/missiles transit the country. This is a
  fundamentally different framing from calendar forecasting:
    - Target: P(region X enters alert in next 15-30 min | neighbouring regions'
      alert state in the last 15-30 min).
    - Build a minute-resolution binary "active alert" panel per oblast, then
      use lagged neighbour states as features (logistic/gradient-boosted).
    - Evaluate with time-based split + AUC/precision-recall, since this is an
      imbalanced nowcasting problem.
  Before committing, FIRST measure the actual cross-region onset lags in the
  data (e.g. typical delay between Kharkiv and Poltava/Kyiv onsets) to confirm
  the spatial signal exists at the chosen horizon.

NEXT STEP #2: EXTERNAL EVENT FUSION
  Join alert spikes with external datasets (documented mass-strike events,
  energy-infrastructure campaigns) to (a) validate the anomaly detector and
  (b) test the holiday hypotheses with proper significance, since the
  in-dataset holiday n is too small alone.

NEXT STEP #3: DURATION / SURVIVAL MODELLING
  Treat censored alerts properly (survival analysis / right-censoring) rather
  than excluding them, especially for front-line oblasts where long alerts are
  real. This would allow honest duration modelling that the current exclusion
  approach deliberately avoids.

NEXT STEP #4: BACKFILL EARLY-WAR DATA
  Optionally splice the volunteer dataset (starts 2022-02-25) to recover the
  first ~3 weeks, with explicit source-comparison checks, if full-war coverage
  matters.

NEXT STEP #5: HOURLY MODEL WITH SPATIAL + RECENT-ACTIVITY FEATURES
  The calendar-only hourly model failed (AUC 0.57). A version adding recent
  national activity and neighbour states (i.e. merging Step 1) is the only
  plausible route to beating the base rate at hourly resolution.


================================================================================
BOTTOM LINE
================================================================================
DEFINITE (well-supported):
  diurnal seasonality, regional concentration, war-phase trend, duration
  heavy tail, and that calendar-based timing forecasting does NOT work.
SUGGESTIVE (data thin):
  holiday effects — New Year up and 24 Feb down are the most credible, but all
  rest on only 3-4 yearly observations.
NOT CONCLUDABLE (insufficient/absent data):
  Luhansk, pre-March-2022, post-Oct-2025, and per-city-hour predictability.
BIGGEST CAVEAT:
  trust no number outside 2022-03-15..2025-10-31; the raw feed both duplicates
  history and collapses after Oct 2025.
MOST PROMISING NEXT STEP:
  spatial nowcasting from neighbouring regions — the one framing the evidence
  suggests could actually predict, rather than merely describe.
